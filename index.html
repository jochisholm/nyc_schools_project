How Do NYC Schools in Your Area Stack Up?

Data on free lunches, or reduced lunches, in public schools is a commonly used proxy by researchers for estimating what percent of the school is living in poverty. It is not a perfect method, as the threshold for free lunches - at least in New York City - can be as high as 130 percent of the poverty rate; or, about $40,000 per year for a family of four.

But since data on a school's poverty rate is a much harder number to come by, many researchers still rely on the free lunch data as being a fair indicator of low-income students.

Low-income students are also the ones who are often short-changed when it comes to funding from school districts. This often means that they have fewer teachers, which ultimately leads to class sizes of 30 to sometimes 40 students.

The students who stand to benefit most from smaller teacher-student ratios are students who are coming from a lower income bracket. For instance, in a study published in 1980, researchers found that students in classes of 18 or less students performed better. This number proved to be even more effective for minority and low-income students, even though they tend to be the ones in schools where the class sizes are sometimes double that of more affluent schools in their area.
